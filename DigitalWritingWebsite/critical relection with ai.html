My Interactions With AI

ChatGPT Scares Me Sometimes

Let me get one thing straight. I do not like AI. As a multi-media journalist, I pride myself on being a “one man band” of sorts. When I’m out on a story, there’s no photographer helping out with the shooting, or an audio tech monitoring my levels—it’s all on me.

I like it that way. It means I don’t leave anything up to chance or external factors. To me, AI is just that—an external factor that I can’t control. Over the course of this project, I noticed that my attempted applications of AI fell into two distinct categories: aggregation and content creation.

Let’s start with the positive. Using AI for aggregation is probably what it’s best at. At its core, generative AI is essentially a predictive text system. It takes in user input, weighs what might come next, and produces an answer based on patterns in large datasets.

Because of this, I found myself using AI like a search engine. Since I’m relatively inexperienced with coding, I would ask questions like “how would I do this?” and get responses compiled from across the internet.

While this worked in many cases, one major problem stood out: lack of context. For example, I had an issue where my images wouldn’t scale correctly on my site. After trying to fix it myself, I turned to AI. I went through nearly 20 different solutions—some even contradicting each other.

Eventually, I pasted my entire file into the chat, which finally solved the issue. The problem was that I had mixed terms from different software versions, and the AI assumed I was using the latest one.

This highlights a major limitation: AI depends heavily on user input. If the input is flawed or lacks context, the output will be too. The real limitation of AI isn’t computing power—it’s us.

The second major use of AI is for creative work, and this is where I have the biggest issue. To me, using AI creatively feels like pulling a slot machine lever. Compared to making something by hand, your control over the result is extremely limited.

When humans create, we iterate. We refine drafts, make decisions, and shape a final product with intention. AI removes much of that process and replaces it with trial and error—generating multiple outputs and picking the best one.

I experienced this firsthand when trying to generate images. I started with a simple prompt: “news reporter shooting a story.” The result wasn’t usable—elements looked unnatural and staged.

I refined the prompt, but the results kept shifting unpredictably. At one point, the reporter was suddenly using a phone camera instead. This randomness reinforced my concern: even with effort, I couldn’t achieve full creative control.

In the end, I returned to using stock images and credited sources—methods that gave me predictable and controllable results.

The one area where I felt comfortable using AI was with subtitles and summaries. Since the output is text, I can easily edit and refine it. However, even here, I feel some loss of authorial intent, especially in pacing and timing.

I’m not completely against AI. I use it for transcription and basic editing tasks. But creative work is different. My work brings me joy—from writing to editing to choosing visuals.

If I relied heavily on AI, I might produce something more polished—but I’d lose the satisfaction that comes from creating it myself. And for me, that tradeoff just isn’t worth it.
