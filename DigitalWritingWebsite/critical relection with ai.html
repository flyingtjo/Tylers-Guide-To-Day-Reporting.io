My Interactions With AI

ChatGPT Scares Me Sometimes

Let me get one thing straight. I *do not* like AI. As a multi-media journalist, I pride myself on being a “one man band” of sorts. When I’m out on a story, there’ s no photographer helping out with the shooting, or an audio tech monitoring my levels, it’s all on me. I like it that way. It means I don’t leave anything up to chance or external factors. To me, ai is just that, an external factor that I can’t control. Over the course of this project, I noticed that my attempted applications of AI fell into 2 distinct categories: Aggregation and Content Creation. While in my opinion AI is clearly more suited for one than the other, both have flaws that I encountered over the course of my work. I want to use this reflection as a way to discuss those flaws from my perspective and why I had difficulty implementing AI into my creative process. Let’s start with the positive, using AI for aggregation is probably what it’s best at. It’s my understanding that at its core, generative AI is essentially a predictive text algorithm, like that little grey bar we have on our phones when we type stuff out. That’s probably a gross oversimplification, but for our purposes I think it’ll suffice. It takes in user input, weighs options of “what comes next” and executes that decision based on the outcome. Due to how large traditional LLM data is, it does a great job at predicting “what comes next” with decent accuracy. Because of this, I found myself using AI in this project like a google search. Because I’m rather inexperienced with coding my own website, I’d ask “how would I do this?” and the AI would respond with the answer it aggregated from sources around the net. While this worked fine in most scenarios, there was one particular problem that stuck out to me and that is the absence of context. For example, I had a bug in my website code where my photos wouldn’t size themselves right for the screen and text below them. I tried to solve it myself to no avail, so I asked ChatGPT for help. I went through what was probably 20 different solutions, with some of them contradictory steps. I tried to give more and more context to my issue, but it was to no avail. I ultimately had to take the easy way out and copy/paste my entire file into the chat window. That finally got me the solution I needed. To put it simply I was mixing up terms from different versions of the software. ChatGPT was giving me solutions on the assumption that I was on the most recent version, when in reality, I wasn’t. Sure, a lot of that could’ve been avoided if I had just sent the file in from the start, but I’ll argue that most casual users won’t interface with AI in that manner. Most people will just ask their question in plain (and probably broken) english and expect the perfect solution to their problem. They will be vastly disappointed. The fact of the matter is this, in our current age the thing that limits AI the most isn’t data centers, processing speeds, or the fact that OpenAI has a stranglehold on the market, it’s *us*. It relies on humans for the initial input, and humans get things wrong (quite often actually). You could have the best aggregation in the world, but if the input (or even data itself ) is flawed, than the outcome will be as well. The other method people use AI for is for creative uses, and here’s where I have the most issue. To me, using AI for creative uses like photos and video is like pulling the lever on a slot machine. Compared to making an image or piece of text by hand, the level of control you have on the output is miniscule. Sure, you can tell the AI to write in a certain style or to make a text more like something else, but the user exhibits very little control over the content itself. When a human (me) writes something it isn’t a “one and done” kind of scenario. We iterate and build upon our already existing work. We make a first draft, then through a process of refinement and decision making come to a final product that is designed with intent and purpose. Let me give you an example. If you’ve ever watched a “Behind the scenes” featurette that came with a movie, show, or game, there’s usually a section that talks about the character design process. They quickly progress through the different iterations of the design before landing on the final one that we know and love. Each change in design is a decision being made by the creative team and those decisions resulted in (what they believe to be) a better product. With AI, we lose that iterative process solely because we have limited control over the fine details of an AI’s output, meaning that the best method of getting a product that fits our needs is to generate a bunch of images and pick the one that fits best. Not only is that iterative process replaced with a scattershot approach, but we lose the ability to create and develop the finer details in our work. In this specific project, I tried to generate some AI photos to go along with the video I was editing. I started with a simple prompt “news reporter shooting a story” to use as a background element. I was given a photo that was not what I was looking for. Elements blended into each other, the reporter held the camera at an awkward angle, and overall the entire thing looked staged (probably because I was using a model that was trained off stock footage). I figured that “Oh, maybe the issue was that I wasn’t specific enough with my prompt.” I then added “taken with a phone” to see if the image would feel less staged if it was taken on a more normal device. In the resulting image, the reporter was instead using a phone camera to do her work. This was that “slot machine” element I was talking about. Sure, I could continuously workshop that prompt and eventually get closer to what I was looking for, spending an unknown amount of time (and credits) to get an image that fits my standard, but even if I spent hours deriving a hyperspecific prompt I would never obtain full authorial control over the output. In the end, I decided to do what I always do and use stock footage or images I get from the internet with credit. The place I did feel comfortable using AI was in the episode summary and subtitles. That was primarily because I can actively edit and adjust the AI output because it’s primarily text. If the result deviates from my intent (like a typo or not recognizing someone’s name) for example, I can correct that issue easily. Still, I can’t help but feel that because I didn’t make the subtitles from scratch from the beginning, authorial intent is still lost. Even if I corrected things like spelling mistakes, other aspects (such as sentence pacing or cue-times) would take far longer to correct than simple text. Even in these smaller, more simpler use-cases authorial intent is lost. Look, I’m no Luddite. I use generative AI to help make interview transcriptions and copy edit grammatical mistakes all the time. It’d be hypocritical for me to bury my head in the sand and say “All AI is evil because I said so,” but let me tell you something. My work gives me joy. From the foundational steps, like writing and editing, to the granular moments like choosing what photo goes where in my video. It’s my enjoyment of the work that led to me choosing such a high effort medium to showcase this project. If I’m being honest, my site would probably look a lot better if I used AI tools. Even template-based website builders like Wix or Adobe Portfolio would probably lead to some form of visual improvement. In doing so, however, I’d lose out on the joy of creation.
