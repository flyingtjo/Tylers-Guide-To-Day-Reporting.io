Ready to learn?

This tutorial is split up into multiple parts, with each focusing on a different aspect of reporting. They are meant to be consumed in numerical order, but if you just want a refresher on a particular element, feel free to jump around.

Each lesson will have a video, text explanations, and some final thoughts. There may also be a few surprises along the way, so don't get complacent.

Every newsroom is different, but you can apply these lessons to almost any reporting job you do (CitrusTV or otherwise).

Informational

I asked why the AI chose this word, and it said the site wasn’t trying to sell the user on anything; it sought to educate the audience, and these words work best. This does fit within the typical parameters for those words. Informational is defined by Merriam-Webster as “containing information,” which my site does. Admittedly, this definition is quite useless because it just takes the “al” out of the word and uses it as a verb. So…let’s try “information” alone. That gives us some actual, processable definition. Merriam-Webster has multiple definitions, but I feel that “knowledge gained from investigation, study, or instruction” fits best. Not only is my website hoping to impart this to its readers, but I myself got the majority of the content on this site from my *own* information. These include lived experience, interviews with others, and textbook material from my time in Newhouse. Overall, the site’s main goal is to inform, and I honestly feel the word fits quite well.

Guide

The next word the AI chose for me is “Guide.” I agree that “guide” works well, for one particular reason. My site is *not* all encompassing. If I were to lay out every single step required to be a good day reporter, I’d need more than just a few videos and interviews. My goal is not to create an instruction manual, but to provide points of focus on a reporter’s own journey. My site does not contain the pinnacle of broadcast journalism that all reporters should strive for, but it does provide the initial steps for reaching that point down the line. I am also not providing exact directions in the slightest. These are merely hints and tactics for improving your reporting. Part of the reason journalism is so difficult (and part of the reasons I wish it paid more) is that we need to approach each story with a different method. If I were to offer a “one size fits all” solution, I’d be doing the worst sin anyone in this industry could ever do: lie.

Experiential

I feel that “experiential” works well for this site because that’s where the majority of my information comes from…experience. I spent 2 semesters being a day reporter and many more as a general assignment reporter (same thing, just without the deadline), so I know what I’m talking about. I also like to add my own experience as examples in my lessons as well. Not only is the information I’m distributing originating from my own experience, but the experience itself (the final stories in particular) is being shown to the user. “Experience” also means the site is interactive. It’s not all on a single web page. There’s a sense of progression as you move through the lessons, and you get a better idea of what you’re learning. Beyond the lessons themselves, the navbar adds another interactive element that allows the user to truly have a flow and experience of their own choosing.

Casual

The last word is “Casual,” and I chose it for a very specific reason: It’s what I want this website to feel like. There are no flashy colors, no moving elements, and no distracting visuals. There’s nothing to take the user away from the content and that was my intent. Not only does this make the build experience easier, but I also find it puts the subject (the audience) at ease. If there’s anything I’ve learned from being a reporter, posturing yourself to be more than what you are is a great way to alienate people. Why should this website pretend to be a super well-developed commercial product when its main goal is to engage with college journalists (who are likely already short on time and attention span)? Don’t get me wrong, it’s a usable site, but the site itself shouldn’t be the focus. The content and the information it holds, however, should.
