My History With The Internet

From Childhood to Today

When it comes to discussing my digital identity, there’s a lot to talk about. My relationship with the internet has changed several times throughout my life and to understand where I’m at now, I need to explain where I came from.

As a child of the early 2000’s, I grew up on the internet. I watched my first youtube video at my friend’s house in the early 2010’s. I played computer games on school computers that, looking back, were ancient. To my 8-year-old mind, however, the dusty CRTs and whirring of the overworked desktop computer, was my gateway to the digital world. At school, I played dubiously educational games, made aesthetically confusing power points, and completed so many reading comprehension quizzes that I can picture the mid-2000’s user interface as clearly as my mother’s face. I didn’t develop much of a “digital identity” though. All my interactions with the internet were purely through consuming media, and not producing or responding to it in a meaningful way.

That doesn’t mean I didn’t have an interest in the digital realm, though. In fact, it was quite the opposite. I have distinct memories of scrolling google images looking for memes of the fist-pumping-baby, I watched minecraft youtubers more than any TV show at the time, and played web-games on numerous websites across the web. Despite my clear interest, I just didn’t have the access required to develop an “identity” for a simple reason: parental oversight. My mom and dad were very cognisant of whatever media I was consuming at the time. For example, I wasn’t allowed to watch Spongebob because it “made kids talk back” according to what my parents heard at the workplace. That watchful eye extended its gaze onto my time spent on the internet, at least when I was at home and in view. My “screen-time” was limited and usually confined to whatever devices my parents were using for work at the time. This not only led to me installing a virus on my father’s pc while trying to install minecraft mods, but having an extremely controlled experience online as well.

In elementary school, it was easy for my parents to keep track of what I was doing online. Since most of the internet-accessable devices were controlled by (or in constant view) of them, they got to act as “gatekeepers” for my consumption of content. I rarely had my own “accounts” for persistent web experiences, and the few I did have were usually registered under my parents’ name and email. That wasn’t always the case though. There were times where I would sneak away from my parents to consume the online content I wanted to watch. In hindsight, I wasn’t viewing anything too scandalous, but I grew up in such a place where saying the word “fart” was a step too far. I watched minecraft youtubers who swore and watched people play video games that would never be allowed under my parents watch. My mother caught me eventually (and gave me a stern talking to) but that indulgence established a few ideas that inspired my later interactions with the online world. Primarily that some activities online..should be kept hidden from the eyes of authority.

My largely supervised access to the internet changed when I became a middle-schooler. As a result of my parents disagreeing with the county’s school board and a recently obtained diagnosis of ADHD, I began to go to a private school. The school prized itself on being “ahead of the curb” and one of the ways it did so (besides rivaling universities in terms of price) was by making iPads part of the student learning environment. Everything from books to homework was done in a digital environment. This was, to put it bluntly, a new experience for me. I went from having heavily supervised access to the online world, to having my own personal device to browse the internet as I pleased, within a very short amount of time. And this sudden growth and me being surrounded by others who were further along on the digital path than me, led to my presence online developing rapidly. By the time I finished my middle school years, I had graduated from “browsing the internet” to having accounts for youtube, reddit, discord, xbox, roblox, minecraft, and countless others that have been lost to time. Despite my rapid growth, I still didn’t actively contribute to the online world. I absorbed a lot of content, and cultivated algorithms that were tailor made to my interests, but I never contributed to that endless sea of information, instead choosing to idly watch from the shore. My friends began to create instagram and snapchat accounts to catalogue their activities. I honestly had no interest in following them. When I got to high school, I eventually did something that I never thought I’d ever do. I began to post.

During my formative years, I was, and still largely am, a massive dork. I had little to no artistic skills (thanks dysgraphia), a physical frame that was more bone than muscle, and a hyperfixation on escapist media (primarily video games). I was the perfect candidate for becoming a dweller of the internet. It started out small. I made a reddit account to improve my meme-ing skills within my friend-group and fell down the rabbit hole of subcultures like airsoft and anime. I had already spent my formative years watching youtubers, but now, with a twitter account, I could interact with their communities. Most importantly, was my discovery of Discord, a platform that, when it’s not leaking military secrets, connected me to like-minded people who I could talk to anytime I wanted. Now, as a senior in college, I probably spend as much time talking with people whose faces I’ve never seen, than people in my own city.

This all comes down to the answer of a singular question: What is my digital identity? I think that’s a really hard question to answer. In the nearly 10 years that I’ve been “active” on the internet, my role has changed from passive observer to active contributor. I have multiple accounts on the same platform. Some for my professional work as a journalist, and others for self-expression. When I got into high school, I actively posted online. I pursued hobbies like voice acting and cosplay under my legal name. Now, that’s changed. When I decided to be a journalist, I had to purge a lot of those parts of “me” from the internet. My old voice acting profile now sits abandoned and under a pseudonym. The only connection from my real world identity to my online one being a few old credits labeled as “Tyler Oldano” and not “Solid Reptile” (my online handle). I’m now in a field that doesn’t value a lot of individuality, especially in our internet presence. My job has a multi-page outline on what I can and can’t say on my social media account, regardless if it has my name or not. Even if I didn’t have my employer looking over my shoulder, something tells me people would take my reporting less seriously if they found out the guy reporting on a memorial for a transgender veteran was the same guy who volunteered to lend his sub-par vocals to cover an anime opening back in high school (for the sake of my mental health I will NOT be linking that video here). But even if I don’t put my name or face behind some of my content anymore, I still make it. My discord and twitter accounts are still called “solid reptile”. While I rarely post, it has a following list, likes, and occasional bookmark that reflect my own interests. The same can also be said about my “news account”. Just because I use it mostly for work, it doesn’t mean it doesn’t contribute to my sense of digital self.

So…what is the through-line in all of this? Why did I ramble for more than a thousand words about my history with the internet with seemingly no purpose? I did it to show a pattern. Even though my interactions with the online world have changed over the course of my life, there’s one element that has stayed roughly the same, a lack of control. There are certainly moments where I wish I could have joined instagram back in middle school, or posted my true thoughts about an issue on my social media accounts, but there’s been some form of control present in my life. At first it was my parents, keeping a watchful eye over my online actions,. During my developing days I had a brief taste of digital freedom to express my interests online under my real name, but when I began to seriously consider a career in journalism I wiped all of that away. Overall, it’s caused this weird phenomena where I’ve rarely had a time where I could live online as myself without fear of consequence. At first it was worrying what my parents would think about my interests, then it was worrying about the repercussions of my future boss. On one hand, that’s the price of my profession and for now, I don’t mind paying it. But the idea of this “personal” and “work” split creates an interesting question, one that I’ve been trying to answer for this entire text. What is my digital identity? Is it the high-school nerd talking about the latest manga chapter he read? Is it the news reporter talking about the age gap in opioid overdose rates? Could it even be the 7 year old logging onto the school computer to make a powerpoint about scorpions? The real answer is…it’s all of them. My digital identity is a life-long bubbling stew of different goals, motivations, and grammatical proficiency, and honestly…I wouldn’t have it any other way.
